What is Generative AI?

Generative AI refers to machine learning systems that create new content — text, images, audio, video, code, or data — by learning patterns from vast training datasets.

Unlike traditional AI that classifies or predicts from existing data, generative models synthesise entirely new outputs that did not exist before — making them transformational for business creativity and productivity.

1
Train — model ingests billions of examples (text, images, code) and learns statistical patterns
2
Represent — knowledge compressed into billions of numerical parameters (weights)
3
Prompt — user provides natural language instruction or context
4
Generate — model produces probabilistically likely, contextually coherent output
Key Distinction: Discriminative vs Generative
Discriminative AI — classifies existing data (spam filter, medical diagnosis, fraud detection)

Generative AI — creates new data that resembles the training distribution (ChatGPT, DALL·E, Sora, GitHub Copilot)

How Large Language Models Work

The Transformer Architecture

All modern LLMs are built on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., Google Brain, 2017 — "Attention is All You Need"). The core innovation is self-attention: the ability to weigh the relevance of every word to every other word in the input simultaneously.

1
Tokenisation — text split into sub-word tokens (~4 chars each). GPT-4 uses ~100,000 token vocabulary
2
Embedding — each token mapped to a high-dimensional vector (e.g., 4096 dimensions)
3
Self-Attention — model calculates which tokens are most relevant to each other across all positions
4
Feed-Forward — non-linear transformation applied to attended representations
5
Prediction — next token predicted from probability distribution; temperature controls creativity

Training Process: RLHF

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
Phase 1: Pre-training on web-scale corpus (CommonCrawl, Books, GitHub, Wikipedia)
Phase 2: Supervised Fine-Tuning on curated human-written examples
Phase 3: Reward model trains on human preference rankings
Phase 4: PPO optimises model against reward signal

Agentic AI: From Assistant to Autonomous Actor

Agentic AI systems can plan, decide, and act across multiple steps without human intervention for each action. They use tools — web search, code execution, database queries, API calls — to complete complex multi-step tasks.

The OODA Loop for AI Agents
Observe → perceive environment state (screen, documents, APIs)
Orient → contextualise against goal and memory
Decide → choose next action from available tools
Act → execute action, observe result, loop until goal complete

AI Hallucination: Understanding & Managing Unreliability

AI hallucination occurs when a model generates confident, plausible-sounding output that is factually incorrect. It is not a bug but an inherent property of probabilistic language models that optimise for coherent text, not factual accuracy.

Why Hallucination Happens

1
Training objective: Models learn to predict likely next tokens — "likely" ≠ "true"
2
Knowledge gaps: If training data lacked information, model fills gaps with plausible-seeming content
3
Recency bias: Knowledge cutoff means recent events are unknown or underrepresented
4
Confidence calibration: Models do not inherently know what they don't know
Real-World Hallucination Incidents
Mata v. Avianca (2023): US lawyer submitted AI-generated legal brief citing 6 non-existent court cases. Fined $5,000 and faced disciplinary proceedings.

Air Canada (2024): AI chatbot invented a non-existent bereavement fare policy. Court ruled airline liable for chatbot misinformation — $1,000+ damages awarded.

Health chatbot (2023): AI-powered symptom checker hallucinated drug dosage information that could have caused patient harm — recalled by healthcare provider.

Mitigation Strategies

📚
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — ground model responses in retrieved source documents. Reduces hallucination by 60–80% for knowledge-intensive tasks (Meta AI Research 2023)
🌡️
Temperature control — lower temperature (0.0–0.3) for factual tasks; higher (0.7–1.0) for creative work. Low temp reduces fabrication frequency
✅
Verification layer — build automated fact-checking pipelines for high-stakes output (legal, medical, financial documents)
📝
Structured outputs — prompt for citations, ask model to express uncertainty ("if you are not certain, say so"), use JSON output with confidence scores
🔍
Human-in-the-loop — maintain human review for high-stakes domains: medical advice, legal documents, financial guidance, safety-critical content

Prompt Engineering Masterclass

Prompt engineering is the practice of crafting input instructions to maximise the quality, accuracy, and usefulness of AI outputs. It is now a core professional skill — equivalent to email writing in the 2000s.

The RTRI Framework

R
Role — assign an expert persona: "You are a senior financial analyst at Goldman Sachs specialising in ESG reporting..."
T
Task — specify the exact output required: "Write a 500-word executive summary of the following Q3 earnings report..."
R
Requirements — define constraints, format, tone, length: "Use bullet points, avoid jargon, target audience is board level, do not include speculation..."
I
Input — provide the actual content/data/context the model should process: "[paste document/data here]"

Advanced Techniques

🔗
Chain-of-Thought: "Think step by step before answering..." — improves accuracy on complex reasoning by 40% (Wei et al., 2022)
📸
Few-Shot: Provide 2–3 examples of desired output format before the task — model mimics the pattern with new inputs
🔄
Self-Consistency: Run the same prompt 3–5 times, take majority answer — reduces hallucination on factual tasks

Good vs Bad Prompts

❌ WEAK PROMPT
"Write something about marketing strategy"
No role, no context, no constraints, no format. Model will produce generic, unfocused output.
✅ STRONG PROMPT
"You are a marketing strategist with 15 years' B2B SaaS experience. Write a 3-point marketing strategy memo for a $2M ARR project management software company targeting construction SMBs in Australia. Format as: 1) Situation, 2) Target Segment, 3) Top 3 tactics with KPIs. Tone: concise and executive. Max 400 words."
Clear role, specific context, defined audience, exact format, constrained length.
7-Part Enterprise Prompt Structure
1. Role → expert persona
2. Goal → what to accomplish
3. Context → background information
4. Audience → who will read/use this
5. Format → structure, length, style
6. Constraints → what to avoid
7. Examples → sample inputs/outputs (few-shot)

RAG: Making AI Smart About Your Business

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) connects an LLM to your private business knowledge — documents, databases, emails, manuals — so the AI answers questions based on your specific information, not just its general training.

How RAG Works
1
Embed: Business documents chunked and converted to vector embeddings, stored in vector database
2
Query: User question converted to embedding, vector database searched for similar chunks
3
Augment: Top relevant document chunks added to LLM prompt as context
4
Generate: LLM answers using both retrieved context and general knowledge — with source citations

Ethical AI: Principles for Responsible Deployment

Ethical AI is not a compliance checkbox — it is a business necessity. Organisations that deploy AI unethically face legal liability, reputational damage, and customer loss. Building ethics into AI from the start is less costly than fixing it after harm occurs.

⚖️
Fairness
AI systems must not discriminate based on protected characteristics. Requires regular bias audits, diverse training data, and disparate impact testing across demographic groups.
🔍
Transparency
Stakeholders should understand when AI is being used and how it influences decisions. "Black box" AI is unacceptable in high-stakes contexts (healthcare, justice, finance).
👤
Accountability
Clear human ownership of AI decisions. Someone must be responsible when AI causes harm. AI systems should have named accountable owners with clear escalation paths.
🔒
Privacy
Data minimisation, consent, right to deletion, and purpose limitation. AI trained on personal data must comply with GDPR, Australia Privacy Act, and applicable local laws.
🛡️
Safety & Reliability
AI must perform reliably and safely. Failure modes must be understood and designed for. Critical systems need human oversight and fail-safe mechanisms.
🌍
Societal Benefit
AI should create broad societal benefit, not concentrate advantage. Consider environmental impact (training GPT-3 used ~552 tonnes CO₂ equivalent), labour market effects, and access inequality.

Representation Bias: Training data underrepresents certain groups. NIST facial recognition study: 100x worse accuracy for Black women vs white men in some models (NIST FRVT 2019)
3
Measurement Bias: Proxy variables that correlate with protected characteristics — e.g., using zip code (proxy for race) in credit scoring
4
Aggregation Bias: Building one model for diverse populations that doesn't work well for any subgroup — common in medical AI trained predominantly on white male patients
5
Deployment Bias: Model used for a purpose different from what it was designed for — a general-purpose LLM deployed as a medical diagnosis tool

Bias Mitigation Techniques

🔬
Diverse training data: Actively curate training sets to include representative samples across all demographic groups. Audit representation before training.
📊
Bias audits: Regularly test model performance across demographic subgroups. Use tools like IBM AI Fairness 360, Fairlearn (Microsoft), Google What-If Tool.
🎯
Fairness constraints: Explicitly constrain model to achieve similar accuracy across groups during training (equated odds, demographic parity).
👥
Diverse development teams: Teams with varied backgrounds are more likely to identify potential harms before deployment (research: diverse ML teams reduce bias by 35%).
🔄
Ongoing monitoring: Bias can emerge over time as the world changes. Deploy bias monitoring as continuous process, not one-time assessment.

Agentic AI Explosion

Business implication: AI moves from information retrieval to genuine problem-solving for complex analytical and strategic tasks.

AI-Native Software

On-Device AI

📋 Week 1: AI Audit
1. List all AI tools currently in use (sanctioned + shadow IT)
2. Identify the top 5 highest-volume, lowest-skill tasks in your team
3. Survey team on AI confidence (1–5 scale) and biggest concerns
4. Review competitor AI activity (LinkedIn, press releases, job ads)
5. Document: What data do you have? Where is it? What quality?
🎯 Month 1: Quick Wins
1. Deploy Microsoft Copilot or Google Gemini for your team (email + meeting summaries)
2. Create a team prompt library (10+ tested prompts for common tasks)
3. Automate one repetitive workflow with Zapier AI or Make.com
4. Run a 2-hour AI tools workshop for your team
5. Define your AI Policy: what data can/cannot go into external AI
🗓️ Quarter 1: Foundation Building
1. Identify AI Champion(s) in your team/org
2. Select 1–2 priority AI use cases based on value/effort matrix
3. Build business case with ROI estimates for top use cases
4. Establish AI governance committee (or join existing)
5. Complete team AI training (all at Literacy Level 2 minimum)
📈 6-Month Milestones
1. 1 AI use case in production (not just pilot)
2. Measurable ROI documented and presented to leadership
3. AI tool usage rate >70% of target team
4. AI roadmap approved for next 12 months
5. 1 AI case study published internally (for culture building)
🏆 12-Month Milestones
1. 3+ AI use cases in production
2. Annual AI ROI exceeds AI spend by 2x minimum
3. All employees at AI Literacy Level 2
4. AI integrated into core business processes (not separate "AI projects")
5. AI strategy reviewed and updated for next year
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid
❌ No executive sponsor
❌ Starting without a data strategy
❌ Piloting 10 things, scaling none
❌ Measuring activity (hours of training) vs outcome (productivity)
❌ Ignoring change management
❌ No AI governance framework before deployment
